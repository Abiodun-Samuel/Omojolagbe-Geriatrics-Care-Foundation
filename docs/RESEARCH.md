# Phase 1 Research

Sources are listed at the end. Findings are organised as the brief requires: recurring section inventory, trust/proof mechanics, art-direction references, and two things every competitor does that we will deliberately not do.

## 1. Section inventory recurring across top providers

## 2. Copy patterns and proof mechanics that build trust in care

Trust is decided before price. The mechanics that recur:

- **Name the regulator.** In Nigeria the two credible references are:
  - **NMCN: Nursing and Midwifery Council of Nigeria** (licenses nurses/midwives). "Nurses registered with the NMCN" is a concrete, checkable claim.
  - **HEFAMAA: Health Facilities Monitoring and Accreditation Agency** (Lagos State), which registers and accredits home-care facilities and *defines* home care as "health care services in the home of the patient, especially for patients who are chronically ill, elderly or disabled." Useful framing language and a real bar (registered nurse + nurse aides + supervising medical practitioner + ambulance + emergency kit). We reference HEFAMAA as the standard we build to as we expand into Lagos, without claiming accreditation we do not hold.
- **Make vetting visible as a funnel**, not an adjective: application → background/criminal check → reference checks → health screening (e.g. TB) → skills + scenario assessment → training/certification → supervised placement. Homage publishes exactly this sequence.
- **Show the process end to end** so an anxious buyer knows what happens after they click: assessment → caregiver matching → written care plan → ongoing supervision → visit reports to family.
- **Family visibility as a promise:** "a care report after every visit," visit notes, vitals, medication support. Cera and Homage both convert on this.
- **Plain language over clinical jargon** (Home Instead: "help after a hospital stay," not "post-acute care transition"). Our audience is an adult child deciding for a parent, often anxious, sometimes abroad.
- **Concrete counts as proof:** years active, families/elderly served, cities, number of caregivers trained. We have "since 2018" and "1000+ elderly."
- **Careers page doubles as a trust signal:** showing hiring standards reassures care buyers that carers are screened.

## 3. Diaspora insight (our differentiator, researched specifically)

- Adult children abroad arranging care for parents in Nigeria is "one of the fastest-growing segments of demand" for home care in the country.
- Working models quote concrete naira subscriptions (e.g. ~₦25,000/month for home visits + vitals + prescription management; ~₦35,000 adding "doctor on demand"). This validates our indicative-range approach.
- Diaspora families specifically need: a **24/7 line reachable outside Nigerian business hours, including WhatsApp**; the ability to **pay from abroad in naira**; and **verifiable, reported care** (they cannot see it themselves).
- The Nigerian diaspora remits **$20B+ annually**, healthcare a top expense, but funds usually arrive *after* an emergency. Positioning: arrange structured care *before* the crisis. This is the emotional spine of the `/diaspora` page.

## 4. Three art-direction references (motion, type, layout: not colour)

1. **Editorial / Editorial-New-font sites on Awwwards (typography-driven, asymmetric grid, generous negative space).**
   - Steal: large characterful serif display set against a disciplined text grid; whitespace as confidence; type as the hero rather than a photo.
   - Avoid: purely aesthetic type that hurts readability for an older-leaning, anxious audience; overlong measure.
2. **Cera / Homage (health B2C with a family-app spine).**
   - Steal: the "family visibility / report after every visit" narrative rendered as a clear, calm product story; conversion-focused clarity without coldness.
   - Avoid: app-store gloss and stocky "smiling nurse" hero; feature-list overwhelm.
3. **Typography-driven "reveal on scroll" motion sites (Awwwards inspiration set).**
   - Steal: one orchestrated hero reveal and restrained scroll-reveal of headings; motion that clarifies hierarchy.
   - Avoid: motion that blocks reading, parallax that fights scroll, anything that fires twice or ignores `prefers-reduced-motion`.

## 5. Two things every competitor does that we will deliberately NOT do

1. **Stock "smiling white nurse with clipboard" hero photography.** Nearly universal and instantly makes a Nigerian site feel imported. We use only real Nigerian/African subjects (we already own 39 such photos) and lead the hero with type + our own imagery, not a stock smile.
2. **A rotating hero carousel / slideshow of vague promises.** Common (including the current site) and it dilutes the thesis and hurts performance and accessibility. We commit to one clear thesis hero with a single primary action. (Second habit we also reject: burying the price behind a "contact us" wall with zero indication: we give honest indicative ranges instead.)
